A Look at Hip-Hop's Coast Rivalry

TK TK TK TK

By: Matthew Conlen

In the 1990s hip-hop was dominated by a rivalry between artists on the East and West coasts of the United States. Tupac Shakur and Dr. Dre’s Death Row Records made a distinct brand of California rap, while Notorious B.I.G. championed the scene in New York. Tragically, the rivalry turned quiet after both Tupac and Biggie lost their lives in drive-by shootings.

Today, hip-hop is more popular than ever and a new generation of rappers and producers are constantly experimenting with new sounds and flows. While southern cities like Atlanta and New Orleans boast a number of talented MCs, the biggest crews are still on the coasts in New York and Los Angeles. In an effort to dig deeper into the history of the genre, I wanted to see if I could chart the influence of rappers hailing from the different epicenters.

To do so, I scraped data from the Billboard’s Hip-Hop / R&B Chart, going all the way back to the chart’s inception in 1988. This gave me a monthly breakdown of the genre’s top 100 songs, including the track’s title and artist. I cross-referenced the artist names with Wikipedia’s lists of rappers associated with the East Coast, West Coast, and the southern “Third Coast.” By combining the sources, I was able to estimate an index of the popularity each region’s artists over time.
